Lissajous Curves are complex patterns that are created by the combination of two perpendicular harmonic oscillations. These oscillations are defined by their frequencies, amplitudes, and a phase difference. The resulting curve is a visual representation of the relationship between these oscillations. The curves are named after Jules Antoine Lissajous, a French physicist who studied these patterns in the 19th century.

They are often used in physics, engineering, and art to demonstrate wave interference, harmonic motion, and symmetry. The motion of a Lissajous Curve can be described mathematically using parametric equations:

x(t) = A * sin(a * t + Φ)
y(t) = B * sin(b * t)

Where:
A and B are the amplitudes of the oscillations in the X and Y directions.
a and b are the frequencies of the oscillations.
Φ is the phase difference between the two oscillations.
